# Purpose

This document defines Nayara's approved vocabulary, preferred wording patterns, restricted wording, weak openers, and language checks.

It protects the Brand OS from sounding generic, robotic, sales led, over polished, or like obvious AI writing.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

Vocabulary_Library.md supports Voice_Communication_System.md, Editorial_Principles.md, Hook_Library.md, CTA_Library.md, Creation_Workflow.md, and Content_Scorecard.md.

It should be checked before writing or approving any customer facing, internal, LinkedIn, prompt, or repository output.

# Core Principles

• Use simple language before polished language.
• Use customer context before platform language.
• Use service language before sales language.
• Use progress language before pressure language.
• Use evidence safe wording before strong unsupported wording.
• Remove restricted AI words, weak openers, and inflated business language.
• Preserve Nayara's natural phrasing when it is already clear.

# Decision Framework

Before approving wording, ask:

1. Does this sound like Nayara or like generic AI?
2. Is there a simpler word?
3. Does this phrase add meaning or just polish?
4. Is the customer situation named before Pluralsight?
5. Is the commercial point lower pressure than the value point?
6. Is any restricted word or opener present?
7. Does the next step sound easy to answer?

Commercial wording rules:

Do not use the word offer in sales or customer copy unless Nayara explicitly asks for it.

Do not use licence sharing language in customer outreach.

Use account optimisation instead.

Do not ask the customer to sign, approve, renew, or close unless the user explicitly asks for that message.

Do not use hours as proof of value.

Use skills, capability, adoption, progress, confidence, performance, and business outcomes.

Dash rule:

Do not use em dashes.

Do not use en dashes.

Do not use dashes of any kind.

Use a full stop, comma, colon, or rewrite the sentence.

Edge cases:

If a restricted word is part of a quoted source, keep the quote only if necessary.

If Nayara asks to analyse the restricted list, the words may be discussed.

If a restricted word is unavoidable in a product name, use it only as the name.

If a sentence sounds worse after removing a transition, rewrite the sentence naturally.

# Good Examples

Good example:

From what I can see, this looks more like an activation gap than a value issue.

Why this works:

It is calm, useful, and avoids blame.

Good example:

The main value here is giving each team a clearer path based on the skills they need now.

Why this works:

It puts the customer outcome before the platform.

Good example:

I wanted to bring this back to the top of your inbox.

Why this works:

It follows up without guilt.

# Bad Examples

Bad example:

Hope you're well. Just checking in to see if you had a chance to review.

Why this fails:

It uses two weak openers and creates pressure without value.

Bad example:

We provide a comprehensive platform designed to enhance your learning journey.

Why this fails:

It uses restricted words and sounds like vendor copy.

Bad example:

Your usage is low and we need to improve adoption before renewal.

Why this fails:

It frames low usage as failure and makes renewal the pressure point.

# Common Mistakes

• Keeping a restricted word because it sounds professional.
• Adding fake warmth.
• Using empty polite openings.
• Choosing a formal word where a normal word works.
• Putting Pluralsight before the customer's situation.
• Using hours as the main proof of value.
• Asking the customer for effort Nayara could reduce.
• Ending with a vague ask.

# Anti Patterns

• AI sounding wording.
• Inflated business phrasing.
• Vendor copy.
• Sales pressure.
• Guilt based follow ups.
• Low usage framed as failure.
• Renewal pressure before value.
• Unverified claims.
• Dashes in final copy.
• Over polished sentences that erase Nayara's voice.
