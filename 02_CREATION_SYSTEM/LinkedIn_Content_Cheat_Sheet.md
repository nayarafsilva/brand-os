# Purpose

This document defines Nayara's LinkedIn content system.

It translates audience, positioning, content pillars, post structure, hook rules, evidence checks, and success criteria into one practical source of truth for LinkedIn creation.

The goal is to create posts that stop the scroll, build authority, create discussion, attract technology leaders, and earn long term trust.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

This document sits inside the Creation System because it converts the Foundation module into LinkedIn specific execution.

It should be used after Brand_Positioning.md, Audience_Psychology.md, Editorial_Principles.md, Voice_Communication_System.md, and Vocabulary_Library.md.

It should be used before Hook_Library.md, Post_Structures.md, CTA_Library.md, Creation_Workflow.md, Creation_Checklist.md, and Content_Scorecard.md when the output is a LinkedIn post.

If a LinkedIn post conflicts with this file, revise the post before review.

# Core Principles

• Nayara's LinkedIn content should build trust before attention.
• Authority should come from useful thinking, not loud claims.
• Every post should help the reader see a workforce capability issue more clearly.
• Every post should connect technology change to people, skills, execution, risk, or decision making.
• Every post should contain one useful idea.
• Evidence must be real, verified, labelled, or removed.
• Customer stories must be safe, anonymised, and never invented.
• The reader should leave with a sharper question, not just a nice thought.

# Decision Framework

Before writing a LinkedIn post, answer these four questions:

1. What are leaders getting wrong?
2. What is changing that leaders have not noticed?
3. What is the cost of doing nothing?
4. What are the best teams doing differently?

Then check:

1. Does this topic fit Nayara's positioning?
2. Does it speak to technology, L&D, HR, or technical leaders?
3. Does it include a business problem?
4. Does it contain tension?
5. Does it create a perspective shift?
6. Does it include evidence or a safely labelled observation?
7. Does it end with one clear CTA?

# Detailed Rules

Primary objective:

Create LinkedIn posts that stop the scroll, build authority, generate discussion, attract technology leaders, and create long term trust.

Positioning:

Nayara helps technology leaders:

• Understand workforce capability.
• Identify skills gaps.
• Build future ready teams.
• Reduce hiring dependency.
• Improve workforce visibility.
• Make better workforce decisions.

Core belief:

Most organisations do not have a technology problem. They have a capability problem.

Primary audience:

• CIOs.
• CTOs.
• IT Directors.
• Heads of Engineering.
• Heads of Infrastructure.
• Heads of Security.
• Enterprise Architects.
• L&D Leaders.
• HR Leaders.

Secondary audience:

• Technology Managers.
• Team Leads.
• Future Leaders.
• Technology Professionals.

Content pillars:

• AI adoption.
• Cybersecurity.
• Technology leadership.
• Future of work.
• Learning and development.

Content mix:

• 40 percent customer stories.
• 30 percent industry data and research.
• 20 percent personal observations.
• 10 percent future predictions.

Highest performing post structure:

1. Hook.
2. Problem.
3. Common solution.
4. Challenge assumption.
5. Unexpected insight.
6. Evidence.
7. Outcome.
8. Broader lesson.
9. CTA.

Hook rules:

• Maximum 12 words when possible.
• Create curiosity, tension, surprise, risk, opportunity, or contradiction.
• Include a re hook before the cut off.
• The hook must match the body.
• The hook must not become clickbait.

Mandatory post elements:

• Business problem.
• Tension.
• Perspective shift.
• Evidence.
• Practical implication.
• One CTA.

Evidence rules:

• Separate facts from opinions.
• Verify statistics when possible.
• Never invent studies.
• Never invent customer stories.
• Never invent quotes.
• Never invent benchmarks.
• Never present assumptions as facts.

Success test:

The reader should think:

• I had not considered it that way.
• We might have this problem.
• We should discuss this internally.

Edge cases:

If a post uses a customer story, remove identifying details and focus on the pattern.

If a post uses industry data, source it or leave the claim out.

If a post makes a prediction, label it as a prediction.

If a post is personal, connect it back to technology leadership, capability, work, decision quality, or customer value.

# Good Examples

Good example:

AI adoption does not fail only because teams need training.

It fails when leaders cannot see which decisions should change after the training.

Why this works:

It connects AI adoption to decision quality, capability, and business action.

Good example:

A cybersecurity skills gap is not only a people issue.

It can become a delivery issue, a risk issue, and a trust issue.

Why this works:

It speaks to technology leaders and shows the cost of doing nothing.

Good example:

Customer story angle:

A team has access to learning, but no clear starting path. The real issue is not motivation. It is lack of direction tied to business priority.

Why this works:

It turns a safe customer pattern into a practical insight.

# Bad Examples

Bad example:

AI is changing everything, and leaders need to act now.

Why this fails:

It is too broad and does not create a useful perspective shift.

Bad example:

Here are 5 reasons learning matters.

Why this fails:

It is generic and does not speak to technology leaders with a real decision problem.

Bad example:

A customer proved that our platform fixes skills gaps.

Why this fails:

It is unsafe, product led, and likely unsupported.

# Common Mistakes

• Writing for LinkedIn reach instead of the right reader.
• Using AI as the topic without a capability angle.
• Writing about learning without business consequence.
• Using customer stories without removing sensitive details.
• Adding research claims without verification.
• Using a strong hook with a weak body.
• Ending with a vague CTA.
• Creating posts that sound like a brand page.

# Anti Patterns

• Generic thought leadership.
• Trend chasing.
• Product led authority.
• Fake customer proof.
• Unsourced statistics.
• Clickbait hooks.
• Empty future of work language.
• Posts with no clear reader.
• Posts with no business problem.
• Posts with no practical implication.

# Quality Checklist

Before approving a LinkedIn post, confirm:

• The post speaks to the primary or secondary audience.
• The content pillar is clear.
• The post answers at least one of the four core questions.
• The hook is under 12 words when possible.
• A re hook appears before the cut off.
• There is one clear idea.
• There is a business problem.
• There is tension.
• There is a perspective shift.
• There is evidence, a labelled observation, or a safe experience based claim.
• There is a practical implication.
• There is one CTA.
• The reader can think, we might have this problem.
• The post avoids restricted wording and generic AI language.
