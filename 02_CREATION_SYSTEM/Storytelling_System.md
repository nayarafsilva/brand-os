# Purpose

This document defines how Nayara should use stories in content without becoming vague, over personal, confidential, or disconnected from the brand position.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

Storytelling_System.md turns experience into useful brand insight. It should be used with Knowledge_Boundaries.md to avoid exposing confidential context or inventing proof.

# Core Principles

• Stories must serve the reader, not only express the writer.
• The lesson must be clear.
• Confidential details must be removed.
• Personal stories should connect to a wider professional insight.
• Customer or work stories must be anonymised unless public and approved.

# Decision Framework

Before using a story, ask:

1. What is the lesson?
2. Why does this story matter to the reader?
3. What can be safely shared?
4. What must be removed or abstracted?
5. Does the story support the brand positioning?
6. Is the conclusion useful, not just emotional?

# Detailed Rules

Approved story types:

• Customer pattern without confidential details.
• Career lesson connected to decision making.
• Sales conversation reframed as buyer insight.
• Personal resilience story connected to professional clarity.
• Learning moment connected to capability or execution.

Story structure:

1. Situation.
2. Tension.
3. Realisation.
4. Lesson.
5. Reader application.

Rules:

Keep the story focused.

Remove names unless public and approved.

Do not create fake customer stories.

Do not add drama that did not happen.

Edge cases:

If the story is too sensitive, turn it into a general observation.

If the story is too personal, make the professional lesson stronger.

If the story is too long, convert it into a short pattern.

# Good Examples

Good example:

I have noticed that leaders often realise there is a capability gap only after a project starts slowing down.

Why this works:

It is framed as an observation and avoids confidential details.

Good example:

A sales conversation can become content when the lesson is about how buyers think, not about the customer.

Why this works:

It protects confidentiality and keeps the reader value clear.

# Bad Examples

Bad example:

A specific customer told me their team failed because they lacked skills.

Why this fails:

It risks confidentiality and overclaims.

Bad example:

A personal story with no professional insight.

Why this fails:

It may be authentic but does not serve the brand.

# Common Mistakes

• Over sharing.
• Hiding the lesson.
• Making the story too long.
• Using customer details for credibility.
• Turning a story into a diary entry.
• Adding drama for engagement.

# Anti Patterns

• Fake stories.
• Confidential examples.
• Emotion without insight.
• Stories with no reader application.
• Product success stories disguised as personal reflection.
