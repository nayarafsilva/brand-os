# Purpose

This document defines how Nayara should write hooks that attract the right audience without becoming clickbait, vague, or generic.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

Hook_Library.md should be used before drafting LinkedIn posts, carousel openings, newsletter introductions, and short form commentary. It depends on Foundation documents because hooks must serve positioning, not just attention.

# Core Principles

• The hook should create useful tension.
• The hook must serve the post, not trick the reader.
• Specific hooks beat broad hooks.
• Commercial tension beats generic inspiration.
• The best hook names a problem the reader already feels but has not fully articulated.

# Decision Framework

Before approving a hook, ask:

1. Does it speak to the right audience?
2. Is there tension, contrast, or specificity?
3. Does it connect to the core idea?
4. Is it clear on mobile?
5. Does it avoid engagement bait?
6. Would the rest of the post fulfil the promise?

# Detailed Rules

Approved hook types:

Contrast hook:

Show the difference between what people think and what is actually happening.

Risk hook:

Name a hidden business risk.

Reframe hook:

Take a familiar topic and change the lens.

Observation hook:

Share a pattern from experience without pretending it is formal research.

Question hook:

Ask a question that exposes a decision problem.

Rules:

Keep the hook short.

Avoid trying to sound clever.

Avoid fake urgency.

Avoid exaggerated certainty.

Make the reader feel seen, not manipulated.

Edge cases:

If the hook mentions AI, connect it quickly to capability, adoption, decision quality, or business risk.

If the hook is personal, make the professional lesson clear.

If the hook is provocative, ensure the post supports the claim.

# Good Examples

Good example:

AI readiness is not a training problem first. It is a decision problem.

Why this works:

It reframes a familiar topic in Nayara's core lane.

Good example:

The skills gap usually becomes visible after it has already become expensive.

Why this works:

It creates risk based tension.

Good example:

Your learning strategy is only as strong as the business decision it helps people make.

Why this works:

It connects learning to business impact.

# Bad Examples

Bad example:

AI is changing everything.

Why this fails:

It is too generic.

Bad example:

You will not believe what leaders are missing.

Why this fails:

It is engagement bait.

Bad example:

The future of work is here.

Why this fails:

It is overused and vague.

# Common Mistakes

• Writing hooks that are too broad.
• Starting with a trend instead of a tension.
• Creating drama the post cannot support.
• Writing for clicks instead of authority.
• Making every hook sound the same.

# Anti Patterns

• Clickbait.
• Fake urgency.
• Generic AI hooks.
• Hooks that overpromise.
• Hooks with no connection to the post.
• Hooks written for everyone.
