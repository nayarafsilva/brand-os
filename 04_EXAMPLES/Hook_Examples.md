# Purpose

This document stores approved hook examples for Nayara's brand. It gives future content creation a reliable set of patterns without forcing every hook to be rewritten from scratch.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

Hook_Examples.md is the examples companion to Hook_Library.md and Re_Hook_Library.md. It should be used after the hook rules are understood, not as a replacement for strategy.

# Core Principles

• Hooks should create useful tension.
• Hooks should speak to decision makers, not everyone.
• Hooks should connect technology change to capability, execution, or risk.
• Hooks should avoid clickbait.
• Hooks should be short enough to work on mobile.

# Decision Framework

Before reusing or adapting a hook, ask:

1. What is the post about?
2. Who is the reader?
3. What tension does the hook create?
4. Does the body of the post fulfil the promise?
5. Does the hook sound like Nayara?

# Detailed Rules

Approved hook categories:

AI readiness hooks:

• AI readiness is not a training problem first. It is a decision problem.
• AI does not expose a technology gap first. It exposes a capability gap.
• The question is not whether your teams need AI training. It is what decisions they need to make better.

Skills gap hooks:

• The skills gap usually becomes visible after it has already become expensive.
• Capability gaps rarely announce themselves as capability gaps.
• Sometimes the skills gap looks like a delayed launch, not a learning problem.

L&D credibility hooks:

• L&D earns trust when it can explain which business risks become smaller.
• Learning strategy is only as strong as the business decision it improves.
• More content does not mean more capability.

Leadership decision hooks:

• The most expensive capability gap is often the one leaders cannot see yet.
• Workforce readiness is a business decision before it is a learning initiative.
• Skills do not matter because learning is nice. They matter because execution depends on them.

Edge cases:

If a hook feels too strong, soften the claim rather than removing the tension.

If a hook feels generic, add the reader's business consequence.

If a hook sounds like a vendor, remove product language.

# Good Examples

Good example:

AI readiness is not a training problem first. It is a decision problem.

Why this works:

It is short, clear, and aligned with Nayara's positioning.

Good example:

The skills gap usually becomes visible after it has already become expensive.

Why this works:

It creates business risk tension without exaggeration.

# Bad Examples

Bad example:

AI is changing everything.

Why this fails:

It is generic and gives no specific point of view.

Bad example:

You will not believe this skills mistake.

Why this fails:

It is clickbait and damages trust.

# Common Mistakes

• Reusing hooks without checking the post idea.
• Choosing the most dramatic hook instead of the clearest one.
• Forgetting the reader.
• Using generic AI language.
• Making the hook sound like a product campaign.

# Anti Patterns

• Clickbait hooks.
• Trend only hooks.
• Hooks with no business consequence.
• Hooks that promise more than the post delivers.
• Hooks that could belong to any AI commentator.
