# Purpose

This document stores approved CTA examples for Nayara's brand. It helps content end with useful reflection, decision prompts, or practical next steps without relying on weak engagement bait.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

CTA_Examples.md supports CTA_Library.md. It should be used after the content idea and structure are clear.

# Core Principles

• CTAs should extend the insight.
• CTAs should respect senior readers.
• CTAs should invite useful reflection or conversation.
• CTAs should avoid empty comment prompts.
• CTAs are optional when the final line already works.

# Decision Framework

Before using a CTA, ask:

1. What should the reader think or do next?
2. Does the CTA match the post type?
3. Is the CTA useful enough to deserve space?
4. Does it avoid engagement bait?
5. Does it sound like Nayara?

# Detailed Rules

Approved CTA examples:

Reflective CTAs:

• Where is the capability gap already showing up in your delivery rhythm?
• What is one decision your team would make better if the right capability existed?
• Which risk is your current learning strategy actually reducing?

Decision CTAs:

• Before adding another AI workshop, ask what business decision it should improve.
• Start by identifying where the gap is already slowing execution.
• Look for the place where training activity is high but behaviour has not changed.

Conversation CTAs:

• I am curious where leaders are seeing this show up first: delivery, adoption, security, or customer impact.
• What would make capability easier to discuss at executive level?
• Which skills gap is easiest to underestimate until it becomes expensive?

Practical CTAs:

• Use this question in your next planning conversation: which business risk becomes smaller if this capability improves?
• Map one strategic priority to the capability it depends on.
• Ask whether your current learning metric proves activity or business readiness.

Edge cases:

If the CTA feels forced, remove it.

If the CTA asks for comments, make sure the question is worth answering.

If the post is emotional, use a reflective CTA rather than a commercial one.

# Good Examples

Good example:

Before adding another AI workshop, ask what business decision it should improve.

Why this works:

It gives the reader a practical decision filter.

Good example:

Which risk is your current learning strategy actually reducing?

Why this works:

It connects learning to executive relevance.

# Bad Examples

Bad example:

Thoughts?

Why this fails:

It is lazy and generic.

Bad example:

Comment YES if you agree.

Why this fails:

It is engagement bait.

# Common Mistakes

• Adding a CTA to every post.
• Asking a vague question.
• Ending with a product push.
• Asking for agreement instead of reflection.
• Making the CTA longer than the insight.

# Anti Patterns

• Engagement bait.
• Generic questions.
• Product push CTAs.
• Forced comments.
• CTAs that weaken strong endings.
