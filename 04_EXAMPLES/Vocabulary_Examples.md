# Purpose

This document stores approved vocabulary examples for Nayara's brand. It shows how to replace generic corporate language with clearer, sharper, more brand aligned wording.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

Vocabulary_Examples.md supports Vocabulary_Library.md and Voice_Communication_System.md. It should be used during drafting, rewriting, and tone correction.

# Core Principles

• Replace vague words with concrete consequences.
• Replace vendor language with reader language.
• Replace hype with decision relevance.
• Replace inflated phrasing with clarity.
• Keep language specific to capability, risk, execution, and decision making.

# Decision Framework

Before choosing wording, ask:

1. Is this phrase specific?
2. Does it sound like Nayara?
3. Does it help the reader understand the issue faster?
4. Does it avoid corporate filler?
5. Does it connect to the brand position?

# Detailed Rules

Preferred replacements:

Instead of:

Empower your workforce.

Use:

Build the capability your strategy depends on.

Instead of:

Unlock human potential.

Use:

Make better decisions with the skills your teams already need.

Instead of:

Digital transformation journey.

Use:

The execution work behind technology change.

Instead of:

Future proof your workforce.

Use:

Prepare teams for the decisions the business will need them to make next.

Instead of:

Learning culture.

Use:

A system where learning changes behaviour, decisions, and execution.

Edge cases:

If the original phrase is widely recognised by the audience, keep it only if the sentence makes the consequence specific.

If a phrase sounds too polished, make it more direct.

If a phrase sounds too casual, restore strategic weight.

# Good Examples

Good example:

The issue is not content volume. It is whether learning changes the decisions teams can make.

Why this works:

It replaces generic learning language with decision value.

Good example:

Capability gaps do not stay invisible forever. They eventually show up in delivery.

Why this works:

It turns an abstract problem into a business consequence.

# Bad Examples

Bad example:

We help organisations leverage innovative learning solutions.

Why this fails:

It is generic and vendor led.

Bad example:

Future proof your people for tomorrow's workplace.

Why this fails:

It sounds broad and overused.

# Common Mistakes

• Using polished phrases that say very little.
• Repeating market language without adding a point of view.
• Making the sentence longer instead of clearer.
• Using transformation language without naming what changes.
• Choosing words that sound impressive but do not sharpen the idea.

# Anti Patterns

• Buzzword replacement with different buzzwords.
• Corporate filler.
• Over abstract language.
• Product centred phrasing.
• Generic future of work wording.
