# Purpose

This document stores approved before and after examples that show how weak, generic, sales led, or over polished writing should be rewritten into Nayara's voice.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

Before_After_Rewrites.md demonstrates the rules inside Voice_Communication_System.md, Vocabulary_Library.md, CTA_Library.md, Creation_Workflow.md, and Content_Scorecard.md.

It should be used when correcting emails, follow ups, customer success messages, LinkedIn posts, and internal notes.

# Core Principles

• Preserve Nayara's natural voice.
• Fix what is unclear, too long, too formal, too weak, or too sales led.
• Do not over polish.
• Customer context comes first.
• Value comes before commercial pressure.
• The next step should be simple.
• Restricted wording should be removed.

# Decision Framework

When rewriting, ask:

1. What makes the original weak?
2. Is the issue tone, structure, clarity, pressure, or missing context?
3. What should stay from Nayara's original wording?
4. What should be cut?
5. How can the next step become easier?
6. Does the rewrite sound like a customer partner?
7. Does it avoid restricted wording?

# Detailed Rules

Rewrite patterns:

Weak follow up to useful follow up:

Bring the context back and make the next step easy.

Sales pressure to customer value:

Move the commercial point behind the customer's priority and value.

Low usage to activation reset:

Do not frame the customer as failing.

Product pitch to customer outcome:

Explain what becomes easier for the customer.

Over polished to human:

Cut inflated language and keep the message natural.

Grammar correction:

Fix the sentence lightly and return only the corrected version.

Rules:

Do not add commentary when Nayara asks only for a correction.

Do not make every message sound like the same template.

Do not use weak openers.

Do not end with a vague ask.

Edge cases:

If the original is already strong, make only light corrections.

If the message is sensitive, keep the tone calm and factual.

If the customer needs to act, reduce their effort.

# Good Examples

Bad:

Hope you're well. I am just checking in to see if you had a chance to review the proposal and let me know if you would like to proceed.

Better:

I wanted to bring this back to the top of your inbox.

The proposal is ready from my side, so the main next step is confirming whether the licence count still matches your team's plan.

Would you have 15 minutes this week or next?

Why this works:

It removes the weak opener, avoids guilt, and gives a clear next step.

Bad:

Your usage is low and we need to improve adoption before renewal.

Better:

From what I can see, this looks more like an activation gap than a value issue.

The team has access, but they may need a clearer starting point tied to their current priorities.

Would it be useful if I pulled this into a simple reset plan?

Why this works:

It reframes low usage without blame and leads with value.

Bad:

We provide a complete platform that can support your organisation's learning needs.

Better:

The main value here is giving each team a clearer path based on the skills they need now.

That should make the programme easier to launch, track, and explain internally.

Why this works:

It replaces product led language with customer outcome language.

Bad:

Due to the fact that the licences are inactive, no billing should occur for these users.

Better:

From what I can see, those licences are not active.

I'm checking the billing view now so we can confirm there is no charge linked to them.

Why this works:

It is shorter, clearer, and keeps progress visible.

Bad:

I have not heard back from you and wanted to follow up again.

Better:

Sharing this again in case it got buried.

The main point is still confirming whether the team wants to keep the current licence count or adjust it before the order is finalised.

Would you have 15 minutes this week or next?

Why this works:

It removes pressure and gives a practical reason for the follow up.

# Bad Examples

Bad rewrite:

I hope this email finds you well. I wanted to gently follow up on this important matter.

Why this fails:

It sounds generic and too formal.

Bad rewrite:

Your usage is concerning and may put the renewal at risk.

Why this fails:

It creates pressure and frames the customer negatively.

Bad rewrite:

Our solution can help you unlock the full potential of your workforce.

Why this fails:

It uses restricted wording and sounds like vendor copy.

Bad rewrite:

Let me know your thoughts.

Why this fails:

It is too broad and not useful.

# Common Mistakes

• Rewriting too much.
• Making the message sound corporate.
• Removing Nayara's natural warmth.
• Keeping weak openers.
• Adding sales pressure.
• Over explaining.
• Forgetting the customer context.
• Ending with a broad ask.

# Anti Patterns

• Generic British corporate rewrite.
• Vendor pitch rewrite.
• Guilt based follow up.
• Low usage shame.
• Renewal pressure.
• Fake warmth.
• Over polished grammar correction.
• Rewrite that changes the intent.
