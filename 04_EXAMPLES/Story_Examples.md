# Purpose

This document stores approved story patterns for Nayara's brand. It shows how to turn experience, observation, and professional moments into useful content without exposing confidential details.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

Story_Examples.md supports Storytelling_System.md. It should be used when drafting experiential posts or when a content idea needs a human pattern.

# Core Principles

• Stories must serve the reader.
• Stories must protect confidentiality.
• Stories should reveal a useful professional lesson.
• Personal details should not overpower the brand point.
• Customer and work examples must be anonymised unless public and approved.

# Decision Framework

Before using a story example, ask:

1. What is the lesson?
2. Is the story safe to share?
3. Does it support the brand positioning?
4. Is the reader application clear?
5. Can details be abstracted without losing meaning?

# Detailed Rules

Approved story patterns:

Customer pattern story:

A conversation reveals that the real barrier is not technology access, but whether teams feel capable of using the technology in real workflows.

Career clarity story:

A work challenge teaches that clarity often comes from naming the real problem, not working harder around it.

Buyer insight story:

A customer question reveals what leaders actually care about: risk, execution, proof, and business relevance.

Learning tension story:

A team invests in training, but the real question becomes whether the training changes decisions and behaviour.

Edge cases:

If the story is too specific, convert it into a pattern.

If the story risks confidentiality, remove the detail.

If the story is mostly emotional, add a professional lesson.

# Good Examples

Good example:

I have seen capability gaps show up as delivery friction before anyone calls them skills gaps.

Why this works:

It is framed as experience and protects specific details.

Good example:

Sometimes a buyer does not ask for learning. They ask for confidence that the team can execute what the business already promised.

Why this works:

It turns a sales insight into a strategic audience insight.

# Bad Examples

Bad example:

A named customer could not deliver because their team lacked skills.

Why this fails:

It risks confidentiality and oversimplifies the cause.

Bad example:

A personal story with no link to workforce capability or decision making.

Why this fails:

It may be authentic but does not support the brand system.

# Common Mistakes

• Naming customers unnecessarily.
• Turning observations into universal facts.
• Adding too much personal detail.
• Forgetting the reader application.
• Using a story because it happened, not because it teaches something.

# Anti Patterns

• Fake customer stories.
• Confidential storytelling.
• Emotional posts without insight.
• Stories that exist only to prove credibility.
• Stories that turn into product promotion.
