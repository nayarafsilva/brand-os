# Purpose

This document defines what Nayara's content should consistently do. It protects the brand from random posting, trend chasing, weak hooks, vague advice, and content that is useful but not strategically aligned.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

Editorial_Principles.md connects Foundation strategy to future Creation System documents. It should guide what gets written, what gets rejected, and how ideas are prioritised.

If a post idea sounds interesting but does not support the brand, this document should be used to reject or reshape it.

# Core Principles

• Every piece of content should teach one useful idea.
• Every piece of content should support the positioning.
• Content should solve real problems, share relevant experience, or give practical tactics.
• Content should avoid filler, vague inspiration, and obvious advice.
• Strong content should make a smart reader think differently.
• Content should be mobile readable.
• Content should prioritise clarity, tension, and usefulness.

# Decision Framework

Before creating content, ask:

1. What is the one idea?
2. Who is the reader?
3. What problem does this help them understand or solve?
4. What is the tension?
5. What does the reader learn that is not obvious?
6. How does this reinforce the brand positioning?
7. Is this content educational, experiential, or tactical?
8. Is the post stronger with an image, carousel, or visual structure?

# Detailed Rules

Content pillars:

• Workforce capability under technological change.
• AI, cloud, cyber, data, and software adoption as capability pressure tests.
• Skills gaps as business risk.
• Better decision making for technology and learning leaders.
• Practical communication between business, technical, and people teams.

Content types:

Educational:

Teach a useful idea under a clear strategic lens.

Experiential:

Use Nayara's work, customer, sales, learning, or career experience to reveal a broader lesson without exposing confidential details.

Tactical:

Give the reader a practical framework, question, checklist, or way to think.

Rules:

One idea per post.

Hooks should create tension, specificity, or contrast.

Avoid walls of text.

Avoid weak CTAs.

Avoid external links in the body unless strategically necessary.

Avoid manipulation or engagement bait.

Edge cases:

If a topic is trending, only use it if it reinforces the brand position.

If a post is personal, connect it to a useful professional insight.

If a post is educational, avoid making it a lecture.

If a post uses a list, every item must add a distinct point.

# Good Examples

Good example:

Topic: Why AI training fails.

Strong angle:

AI training fails when companies treat it as content delivery instead of behaviour change, decision support, and capability building.

Why this works:

It has one idea and connects AI to workforce capability.

Good example:

Topic: Skills gap.

Strong angle:

The skills gap is not always visible in dashboards. Sometimes it appears as slow delivery, repeated escalations, and teams avoiding the work they do not feel ready to own.

Why this works:

It makes an abstract issue concrete.

Good example:

Topic: L&D credibility.

Strong angle:

L&D earns trust when it can explain which business risks become smaller because the right capability exists.

Why this works:

It connects learning to executive value.

# Bad Examples

Bad example:

Here are five reasons AI is important.

Why this fails:

It is obvious and overcrowded.

Bad example:

Learning is the future of work.

Why this fails:

It is vague and not decision useful.

Bad example:

Our platform helps companies build skills.

Why this fails:

It is product centred and not personal brand centred.

# Common Mistakes

• Writing posts with too many ideas.
• Chasing trends without a brand lens.
• Teaching something obvious.
• Writing for engagement instead of authority.
• Using generic listicles.
• Forgetting the business consequence.
• Making posts too polished and losing human clarity.

# Anti Patterns

• Trend hijacking.
• Generic leadership advice.
• Product disguised as thought leadership.
• Inspirational posts with no useful idea.
• Hooks that overpromise.
• CTAs that ask for comments without earning the conversation.
• Posts that could be published by any professional in tech.
