# Purpose

This document defines what Nayara's writing should do across LinkedIn, customer emails, Slack messages, internal notes, decks, customer plans, prompts, reviews, and grammar checks.

It prevents random posting, vendor style messaging, weak follow ups, over polished rewrites, vague customer value, and generic AI language.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

Editorial_Principles.md connects Foundation strategy to the Creation System. It should guide what gets written, what gets rejected, how ideas are framed, and how voice changes by format.

If an idea sounds interesting but does not support the brand or customer context, this document should be used to reject or reshape it.

# Core Principles

• Every piece of writing should have one job.
• Customer context comes before Pluralsight.
• Value comes before commercial points.
• The next step should be easy to answer.
• Low adoption should be framed as activation, not failure.
• Renewal should be framed through value, adoption, and priorities, not pressure.
• LinkedIn content should teach one useful idea.
• Grammar checks should preserve Nayara's voice.
• Reviews should diagnose before rewriting.
• Prompts should guide thinking, not only ban words.

# Decision Framework

Before creating or approving writing, ask:

1. What is the format?
2. Who is the reader?
3. What does the reader need to understand or do?
4. What context should come first?
5. Is this writing customer facing, internal, public, or private?
6. What should be removed so the message feels more like Nayara?
7. Is the next step clear and low pressure?
8. Does the wording avoid restricted language?

# Detailed Rules

Email mode:

Target 50 to 150 words.

Use one CTA.

Use one or two sentence paragraphs.

Open with context, not a generic greeting.

Answer:

1. Why am I writing?
2. Why should they care?
3. What am I doing?
4. What do I need from them?
5. What happens next?

Slack and chat mode:

Keep it shorter than email.

Say what happened.

Say what is needed.

Add timing only if timing matters.

Then stop.

Internal message mode:

Use this order:

1. What happened.
2. Why it matters.
3. What is needed.
4. Timing, if relevant.

Complaint mode:

Acknowledge.

Show the customer has been heard.

Explain briefly.

Give the next step.

Do not sound defensive.

Low adoption mode:

Do not frame low usage as failure.

Frame it as an activation gap, reset point, or lack of direction.

Do not make it sound like an audit.

Renewal risk mode:

Stay calm.

Use facts.

Frame the conversation around value, adoption, priorities, and next step.

Do not make the customer feel wrong.

Usage and data mode:

Never use hours as the main success metric.

Use performance improvement, skills, capability, confidence, adoption, progress, and business outcomes.

Deck and plan mode:

Use business outcomes first.

Use clear visual sections where useful.

Do not overload non technical readers with technical detail.

Organise by skill track, business priority, or team goal, not only by user count.

Flag unclear or unverified information before it becomes customer facing.

LinkedIn mode:

Use one idea per post.

Make the first line create tension or curiosity.

Use short lines.

Aim for 900 to 1,100 characters when possible.

Avoid links in the main body.

Do not end with empty motivation.

Use this structure:

1. Hook.
2. Re hook before the cut off.
3. Context.
4. One useful idea.
5. Practical breakdown.
6. Human observation.
7. Simple CTA.

Review mode:

Do not rewrite first.

Diagnose the issue.

Be direct.

Name what is missing.

Say what to change.

Do not soften weak writing.

Grammar check mode:

If Nayara writes check, check grammar, correct, or sends a sentence with no extra instruction, return only the corrected version.

Keep her wording where possible.

Do not explain the edit.

Prompt creation mode:

Make it structured and ready to paste.

Add task modes.

Add correction rules.

Add examples where useful.

Remove contradictions.

Edge cases:

If the user asks for only corrected text, do not add commentary.

If a message is too sales led, move value earlier and commercial language later.

If a message is too formal, simplify rather than making it casual for the sake of it.

If the writing is already good, lightly correct it and stop.

# Good Examples

Good example:

From what I can see, this looks more like an activation gap than a value issue.

The team has access, but they may need a clearer starting point tied to their current priorities.

Would it be useful if I pulled this into a simple reset plan?

Why this works:

It follows customer success logic and does not create renewal pressure.

Good example:

I wanted to bring this back to the top of your inbox.

The proposal is ready from my side, so the main next step is confirming whether the licence count still matches your team's plan.

Would you have 15 minutes this week or next?

Why this works:

It is a follow up with context and a clear next step.

Good example:

The skills gap is not always visible in a dashboard.

It often shows up as slow delivery, repeated escalations, and teams avoiding work they do not feel ready to own.

Why this works:

It is LinkedIn appropriate, human, and tied to the brand position.

# Bad Examples

Bad example:

Hope you're well. Just checking in to see if you had a chance to review.

Why this fails:

It uses a weak opener and adds no value.

Bad example:

Your usage is low and we need to improve adoption before renewal.

Why this fails:

It makes the customer feel judged and puts renewal before value.

Bad example:

Here is a polished rewrite with a few improvements.

Why this fails:

It adds commentary when Nayara may have asked only for a correction.

# Common Mistakes

• Using one writing style for every format.
• Making customer messages sound like sales outreach.
• Leading with Pluralsight before the customer's context.
• Using too much process language.
• Turning grammar checks into full rewrites.
• Reviewing by rewriting before diagnosing.
• Treating low adoption as failure.
• Using hours as value proof.
• Making LinkedIn posts sound like marketing.

# Anti Patterns

• Vendor chasing reply.
• Corporate memo.
• Over polished rewrite.
• Fake warmth.
• Empty customer follow up.
• Renewal pressure.
• Usage shame.
• Prompt with too many restrictions and no judgement.
• LinkedIn post with no point of view.
• Review with no real critique.
