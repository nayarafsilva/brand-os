# Purpose

This document defines how Nayara should be positioned in the market, on LinkedIn, and across future platforms. It gives the brand a clear strategic lane so content does not drift into generic AI, L&D, sales, or SaaS commentary.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

Brand_Positioning.md translates the brand philosophy into a practical market position. It should guide audience selection, content pillars, voice, editorial decisions, and future offers.

If content feels unfocused, this document should be used to check whether the idea still supports the positioning.

# Core Principles

• Nayara helps technology and business leaders make better workforce decisions before capability gaps become business risks.
• The brand sits at the intersection of technology change, workforce capability, commercial risk, and leadership decision making.
• The brand should feel strategic, practical, human, and commercially aware.
• Nayara is not positioned as an AI influencer, HR influencer, L&D commentator, or product evangelist.
• Current work experience can support credibility, but the positioning must remain portable.

# Decision Framework

Use this positioning filter:

1. Is the topic connected to technology change?
2. Is there a capability, skills, talent, or execution angle?
3. Is there a business consequence?
4. Does the idea help a leader see a decision more clearly?
5. Does it sound like Nayara's personal point of view?
6. Could the idea remain relevant outside her current company?

If the idea passes at least five of these checks, it likely fits the brand.

# Detailed Rules

Primary positioning statement:

Helping technology leaders make better workforce decisions before capability gaps become business risks.

Expanded positioning:

Nayara writes about the decisions leaders need to make when technology changes faster than teams can adapt. Her work connects skills, AI, cloud, cybersecurity, data, and digital transformation to the commercial risks that appear when workforce capability is ignored.

The brand should be useful for:

• Technology leaders who need teams to execute faster.
• L&D leaders who need learning to prove business relevance.
• Revenue and transformation leaders who see skills gaps slowing delivery.
• Founders and operators thinking about workforce readiness.

The brand should avoid:

• Becoming a Pluralsight content channel.
• Becoming generic motivational content.
• Becoming tactical LinkedIn growth content.
• Becoming broad career advice without a capability angle.

Edge cases:

If the topic is about sales, connect it to buyer understanding or strategic communication.

If the topic is about personal growth, connect it to decision quality, resilience, or professional clarity.

If the topic is about AI tools, connect it to adoption, behaviour change, or capability risk.

If the topic is about L&D, connect it to business impact rather than training volume.

# Good Examples

Good example:

AI readiness is not measured by how many people attend a workshop. It is measured by whether teams can make better decisions, move faster, and avoid expensive execution gaps.

Why this works:

It links AI, capability, behaviour, and business risk.

Good example:

The most expensive skills gap is not always the one leaders can see. It is the one hidden inside delayed projects, weak adoption, and avoidable escalations.

Why this works:

It gives the reader a sharper way to think about capability.

Good example:

Learning teams do not need more content. They need a stronger link between skills, roles, business priorities, and the risks the board already cares about.

Why this works:

It speaks to L&D without sounding like traditional L&D content.

# Bad Examples

Bad example:

I help companies upskill their workforce.

Why this fails:

It is too broad and sounds like any training provider.

Bad example:

I post about AI and the future of work.

Why this fails:

It is overcrowded and does not define Nayara's unique angle.

Bad example:

I help Pluralsight customers understand the platform.

Why this fails:

It is current role based, not personal brand positioning.

# Common Mistakes

• Making the positioning too close to the employer.
• Making the positioning too broad to own.
• Using future of work language without a sharper claim.
• Speaking to everyone interested in technology.
• Treating audience growth as the primary outcome.
• Confusing professional credibility with brand positioning.

# Anti Patterns

• AI commentator with no decision making angle.
• L&D content with no commercial consequence.
• Product led authority.
• Career content disconnected from the core niche.
• Thought leadership that makes no specific reader feel seen.
• Broad inspiration without strategic usefulness.
