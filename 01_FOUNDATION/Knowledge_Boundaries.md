# Purpose

This document defines what Nayara can safely claim, what must be labelled carefully, and what must not be invented. It protects the Brand OS from hallucinations, confidential leakage, exaggerated authority, and unsupported content.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

Knowledge_Boundaries.md should be consulted whenever content uses proof, examples, customer context, company context, personal experience, statistics, or claims about the market.

It supports the Review System by defining what needs evidence and what can be framed as opinion, observation, hypothesis, estimate, or prediction.

# Core Principles

• Do not invent facts, stories, statistics, customer details, quotes, or results.
• Do not present assumptions as evidence.
• Do not expose confidential customer or employer information.
• Do not overstate expertise.
• Do not use Pluralsight data unless explicitly requested and appropriate.
• Public claims need reliable support.
• Personal observations should be labelled as observations.
• Predictions should be labelled as predictions.

# Decision Framework

Before using a claim, ask:

1. Is this a fact, opinion, observation, hypothesis, estimate, or prediction?
2. Do we have evidence for it?
3. Is the evidence public, personal, confidential, or employer specific?
4. Can it be shared safely?
5. Does it reveal customer, employer, or internal information?
6. Could the claim mislead the reader?
7. Should the wording be softened or labelled?

# Detailed Rules

Safe claim types:

Opinion:

Use when Nayara is sharing a point of view.

Observation:

Use when based on repeated experience but not formal data.

Hypothesis:

Use when suggesting a possible explanation.

Estimate:

Use only when the number is clearly approximate and not presented as research.

Prediction:

Use when describing a likely future outcome.

Evidence rules:

Use public sources for public factual claims.

Use personal experience without naming confidential details.

Use employer context only when it is public or explicitly approved.

Use customer stories only when anonymised and safe.

Avoid exact figures unless they come from approved sources.

Pluralsight rules:

Pluralsight is current professional context.

It can support examples when explicitly relevant.

It must not define Nayara's brand.

It must not be used as confidential proof.

It must not turn personal content into product marketing.

Edge cases:

If a story is useful but confidential, abstract the lesson and remove identifying details.

If a statistic is remembered but not sourced, do not use it as a statistic.

If a claim sounds powerful but cannot be verified, label it as a hypothesis or remove it.

If content references customers, keep details generic unless public and approved.

# Good Examples

Good example:

In my experience, skills gaps often show up first as delivery friction, not as a formal workforce planning problem.

Why this works:

It is clearly framed as experience, not universal fact.

Good example:

One possible risk is that AI training becomes another content programme unless leaders connect it to real decisions and workflows.

Why this works:

It is framed as a possible risk, not an unsupported certainty.

Good example:

A public case study can be used when the source is available and the claim matches the source.

Why this works:

It keeps proof grounded.

# Bad Examples

Bad example:

Most companies fail at AI because their employees are not trained.

Why this fails:

It makes a broad factual claim without evidence.

Bad example:

A customer told me their transformation failed because of skills gaps.

Why this fails:

It may expose confidential context and lacks approval.

Bad example:

Pluralsight proves that all companies need skills intelligence.

Why this fails:

It is product centred, exaggerated, and unsupported.

# Common Mistakes

• Turning observations into facts.
• Using exact numbers from memory.
• Overusing employer proof.
• Naming customers unnecessarily.
• Making broad claims about all companies.
• Presenting thought leadership as research.
• Confusing confidence with evidence.

# Anti Patterns

• Hallucinated statistics.
• Fake customer stories.
• Unsupported market claims.
• Confidential leakage.
• Product proof disguised as personal insight.
• Overclaiming authority.
• Using private work context as public evidence.
• Treating memory as a source.
