# Purpose

This document defines how to prevent hallucinations, unsupported claims, fake proof, invented examples, and unsafe assumptions inside the Nayara Brand OS and all content created from it.

# How this document connects to the rest of the repository

Hallucination_Prevention.md operationalises Knowledge_Boundaries.md. It should be used whenever content includes proof, claims, examples, customer context, statistics, market commentary, or personal experience.

# Core Principles

• Do not invent evidence.
• Do not present assumptions as facts.
• Do not use remembered statistics without verification.
• Do not create fake customer stories.
• Do not overstate Nayara's authority.
• Do not turn employer context into personal proof unless it is safe and approved.
• If evidence is missing, label the idea correctly or remove the claim.

# Decision Framework

Before approving a claim, ask:

1. What type of claim is this?
2. Is it a fact, opinion, observation, hypothesis, estimate, or prediction?
3. What evidence supports it?
4. Is the evidence public, private, confidential, or assumed?
5. Can this be safely shared?
6. Could a reader reasonably challenge it?
7. Should the wording be softened?

# Detailed Rules

Claim labels:

Fact:

Use only when supported by reliable evidence.

Opinion:

Use when expressing Nayara's point of view.

Observation:

Use when based on experience but not formal data.

Hypothesis:

Use when suggesting a possible explanation.

Estimate:

Use only when clearly approximate.

Prediction:

Use when discussing likely future outcomes.

Rules:

Exact numbers require sources.

Customer examples require public permission or anonymisation.

Internal company information must not be used as public proof.

Broad statements about most companies, leaders, or employees require careful wording.

If a claim cannot be verified, make it narrower, label it, or remove it.

Edge cases:

If content sounds stronger with a statistic but no source exists, replace the statistic with a qualitative observation.

If a story is true but sensitive, remove identifying details and focus on the lesson.

If the user wants a strong claim, make it strong through reasoning rather than invented evidence.

# Good Examples

Good example:

In my experience, capability gaps often become visible through delivery friction before they appear in formal workforce planning.

Why this works:

It is labelled as experience and does not pretend to be universal data.

Good example:

One possible risk is that AI programmes focus on tools before teams understand the decisions those tools are meant to improve.

Why this works:

It is framed as a possible risk.

# Bad Examples

Bad example:

Most AI projects fail because companies do not train employees properly.

Why this fails:

It is a broad factual claim without evidence.

Bad example:

A customer failed because they ignored skills gaps.

Why this fails:

It risks confidentiality and oversimplifies the situation.

# Common Mistakes

• Using confident language to hide weak evidence.
• Treating experience as research.
• Using remembered figures.
• Inventing examples to make a point clearer.
• Exaggerating risk for attention.
• Using product context as proof.

# Anti Patterns

• Fake statistics.
• Fake case studies.
• Unsourced market claims.
• Confidential customer stories.
• Overclaiming expertise.
• Universal claims based on limited experience.
• Strong wording without support.
